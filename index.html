The Challenge

A legacy system described as "impossible to track" was failing the healthcare professionals it was meant to serve.

MedLaunch Concepts is a healthcare technology startup developing the DNV Healthcare Portal—a comprehensive platform designed to replace outdated accreditation systems that have long frustrated hospital surveyors and compliance officers.

The existing system was plagued by abandoned forms, manual workarounds, and workflows that felt like obstacles rather than tools. Users frequently gave up mid-task, resorting to pen and paper to get their jobs done.

Our mission was clear: evaluate the new portal's usability and identify the friction points preventing users from completing their critical work—work that ultimately impacts patient safety.

Our Approach

From stakeholder kickoff to actionable recommendations

Phase 01

Stakeholder Discovery

I initiated contact with Robert Dostert, MedLaunch's project manager, to understand the product landscape. The platform serves two distinct user groups: hospital-side users (quality directors, compliance officers) and DNV-side users (accreditation surveyors).

Key insight from our kickoff: users were "not technical, used to pen and paper." This informed our entire approach—we needed to test whether the digital experience could match the simplicity of analog workflows.

Strategic Decision

We focused on the Surveyor Tool component—the most complex and friction-heavy part of the platform—rather than spreading thin across all features.

Phase 02

Participant Recruitment

I designed a screening survey targeting healthcare professionals with accreditation experience (ages 25-64). While we attempted to use Pratt's Private Panels partnership, the specialized nature of our target users made recruitment challenging.

We ultimately recruited 8 participants through professional networks, including two ideal candidates—P3 (Clinical Psychology PhD with healthcare research experience) and P4 (Healthcare Attorney with RN background)—whom I personally interviewed.

Methodological Consideration

While proxy participants (project managers, product designers) couldn't fully represent end users, their experience with regulated workflows and enterprise software provided valuable comparative insights.

Phase 03

Test Design & Execution

I authored a comprehensive testing script with a realistic scenario: participants would set up and conduct an internal audit for the emergency department at a fictitious hospital. This grounded abstract tasks in concrete, believable context.

We employed concurrent think-aloud (CTA) and concurrent probing (CP) techniques across 60-75 minute remote sessions via Zoom. The four core tasks mapped directly to the surveyor workflow: create survey → build agenda → navigate control center → submit findings.

Phase 04

Analysis & Synthesis

After completing all sessions, we conducted affinity mapping to cluster observations into themes. Patterns emerged quickly: terminology confusion, hidden call-to-actions, and inflexible workflows appeared across nearly every session.

I led the synthesis process, reviewing recordings, compiling observational notes, and translating recurring pain points into specific, implementable design recommendations.

Scope Decision

Rather than overwhelm the client with mockups for every minor issue, we focused on four pages requiring major reconstruction: Survey Page, Agenda, and Submissions. When the client informed us they were already addressing dashboard concerns, we pivoted to capture those insights in an "Additional Findings" appendix.

01

Survey Creation: Lost at the Starting Line

The Problem

Users couldn't find where to begin. Despite a visible "Schedule Survey" button, the terminology created confusion—scheduling implies managing deployment time, not creation. The button's nested placement and identical color to table headers made it feel insignificant.

"I'm looking for a 'Start' button..." — P5

Our Recommendation

Implement a stepped workflow indicator showing users exactly where they are in the survey creation process. Replace "Schedule Survey" with "Create Survey" and establish clear visual hierarchy through color differentiation and prominent CTA placement.

02

Agenda Management: The 25% Problem

The Problem

With only 25% task success, agenda management was the biggest failure point. The single-day view lacked temporal context for 3-5 day surveys. Rigid column assignments prevented real-world hand-offs between team members. No inline editing meant constant context-switching.

"I can assign a person to the whole column but I don't know how I can assign a specific event to another person." — P8

Our Recommendation

Add Day/Week/Month view toggles for broader temporal context. Introduce priority star indicators for high-risk items. Enable draggable task cards for dynamic reassignment. Implement quick-edit modals for in-context editing without losing visual context.

03

Submission & Entry Review: Duplicate Confusion

The Problem

"My Submissions" and "Entry Review" tabs confused 4 out of 8 users. The primary CTA didn't communicate its importance. Missing back navigation left users feeling lost. Two simultaneous information displays (table + card) increased cognitive load.

"Those two things sound like the same thing to me." — P7

Our Recommendation

Consolidate into a single "Submissions" tab. Make the primary CTA visually prominent with proper blue fill. Add checkboxes for bulk actions and row-specific action menus. Change table header color to differentiate from interactive elements.

"I like a sleek platform, and it's really aesthetically pleasing to the eye. What I really like about this is having the different columns."

— Participant 3, noting the positive aspects of the interface

The Outcome

From insights to implementation roadmap

Client Response

The MedLaunch team received our findings enthusiastically. Robert Dostert expressed that our solutions aligned with their development priorities and would be prioritized for near-term implementation. The design recommendations provided a clear path forward without requiring fundamental architectural changes.

Recommended Next Steps

Field testing is essential. Since surveyors use this tool while walking through hospitals—often with spotty connectivity—we recommended tablet-specific usability sessions in real clinical environments. The "QuickFind" feature for snapping photos and logging quick observations needs dedicated mobile testing.

Key Learnings

This project reinforced that domain expertise doesn't compensate for interface issues. Even our healthcare-background participants struggled with workflows. It also showed the value of strategic scope management—focusing deeply on critical pain points rather than cataloging every minor issue.

Impact Potential

Implementing these recommendations could realistically move the Product Usability Scale score from 59.4 (Grade D) to the 70-80 range (Grade B-C) within one to two development cycles—a meaningful improvement that translates directly to reduced training time and higher task completion rates for field auditors.
