The Challenge

A legacy system described as "impossible to track" was failing the healthcare professionals it was meant to serve.

MedLaunch Concepts is a healthcare technology startup developing the DNV Healthcare Portal—a comprehensive platform designed to replace outdated accreditation systems that have long frustrated hospital surveyors and compliance officers.

The existing system was plagued by abandoned forms, manual workarounds, and workflows that felt like obstacles rather than tools. Users frequently gave up mid-task, resorting to pen and paper to get their jobs done.

Our mission was clear: evaluate the new portal's usability and identify the friction points preventing users from completing their critical work—work that ultimately impacts patient safety.

Our Approach

From stakeholder kickoff to actionable recommendations

Phase 01

Stakeholder Discovery

I initiated contact with Robert Dostert, MedLaunch's project manager, to understand the product landscape. The platform serves two distinct user groups: hospital-side users (quality directors, compliance officers) and DNV-side users (accreditation surveyors).

Key insight from our kickoff: users were "not technical, used to pen and paper." This informed our entire approach—we needed to test whether the digital experience could match the simplicity of analog workflows.

Strategic Decision

We focused on the Surveyor Tool component—the most complex and friction-heavy part of the platform—rather than spreading thin across all features.

Phase 02

Research Planning & Recruitment

I wrote the research proposal outlining our methodology, objectives, and success criteria. Then I designed a screening survey targeting healthcare professionals with accreditation experience (ages 25-64).

We ultimately recruited 8 participants through professional networks, including two ideal candidates—P3 (Clinical Psychology PhD) and P4 (Healthcare Attorney with RN background)—whom I personally interviewed.

Methodological Consideration

While proxy participants couldn't fully represent end users, their experience with regulated workflows provided valuable comparative insights.

Phase 03

Test Design & Execution

I authored a comprehensive testing script with a realistic scenario: participants would set up and conduct an internal audit for the emergency department at a fictitious hospital.

We employed concurrent think-aloud (CTA) and concurrent probing (CP) techniques across 60-75 minute remote sessions via Zoom.

Phase 04

Analysis & Synthesis

After completing all sessions, we conducted affinity mapping to cluster observations into themes. Patterns emerged quickly: terminology confusion, hidden call-to-actions, and inflexible workflows appeared across nearly every session.

Scope Decision

Rather than overwhelm the client with mockups for every minor issue, we focused on pages requiring major reconstruction: Survey Page, Agenda, and Submissions.

A Note on Design Recommendations

After synthesizing the insights, my team members did a great job mocking up the design recommendations. I made minor edits to elevate the designs, which I highlight in blue (#2563eb) throughout the mockups below.

01

Survey Creation: Lost at the Starting Line

Users were unclear how to get started creating a survey and got easily lost during the multi-step process and navigating the different status types.

Problem 1A: Scheduling vs Creating Confusion

Users were unsure how to create a survey, despite the presence of a "Schedule Survey" button. "Scheduling" actually created confusion as scheduling is generally understood as managing deployment time rather than creation itself. The button was inside two layers of boxes, making it feel less prominent. It's also the same color as the Historical Data table header.

"I'm looking for a 'Start' button..."— Participant 5
"Like there's nothing that's kind of like leading me as to how to use the tool or what to do first."— Participant 1

Insight

Users desired a more explicit survey creation guided experience.

Problem 1B: Form Entry Frustration

Users were used to other form and scheduling software tools, often relying on old learned behaviors to move quickly, resulting in frustration when the designs didn't conform to other tools.

"The UI here is bugging me. You have the word 'days' outside the box? As a user, do I type '3'? Or '3 days'?"— Participant 5

The tool is not automatically connected to a database like Microsoft Teams or Pulse, which allows users to select pre-loaded teammates.

"I click the team member field and it just says 'No options'. Is the database broken? It feels static..."— Participant 6

Insight

Users desired a form entry experience that aligns with common design standards across other tools.

Problem 1C: Status Confusion

Users were confused about the status terminology and what they meant for the survey. Users expected status changes that didn't happen upon taking actions (ex: updating a survey resulted in a draft not an updated live survey).

"Is 'Confirm' the same as 'Start'? To me, 'Confirm' just means 'Yes, this data is correct.' It doesn't imply 'Action.'"— Participant 5
"If I'm scheduling and maintaining multiple surveys, it will be so difficult to differentiate quick and find which one is in draft, which one is currently going on, and which one is upcoming."— Participant 7

Insight

Users want more clarity about status types and when they change, as well as flexibility in editing surveys after confirmation.

Design Recommendations

Solutions

Survey Creation - Main Page

  • Help users understand the workflow of survey creation by making the steps, and what order they occur in, clear
  • Change language and terminology that users found confusing
  • Change colors in order to establish clearer hierarchy and prominence

Survey Creation - Detail Form Entry

  • Help users understand the kind of information they need to provide
  • Guide users using design patterns they are familiar and comfortable with
  • Give users a way to problem solve when they run into issues
02

Agenda Management: The 25% Problem

With only 25% task success, agenda management was the biggest failure point. Obscured timeline controls and inconsistent role labeling hide critical context, increasing the risk of coordination errors.

Problem 2A: Missing Temporal Context

The single-day view lacks the broader temporal context required to identify "High Risk" audit milestones. Surveyors are managing a complex 3-5 day narrative where finding specific milestones requires memorization rather than visual recognition.

"People might enjoy a weekly feature and being able to rank things in terms of importance - like starring a task"— Participant 3

Insight

Without a Weekly/Monthly view or "Priority Star," surveyors cannot visually strategize their time across the full survey duration.

Problem 2B: Rigid Column Assignments

The system assumes a static one-to-one relationship between a surveyor and a track. In reality, audit teams act as a dynamic mesh where responsibilities shift hour by hour.

"I can assign a person to the whole column but I don't know how I can assign a specific event to another person in absence of the person to whom the whole column is assigned."— Participant 8

Insight

The UI's rigid "Whole Column" assignment prevents mandatory compliance flexibility when surveyors need to cover for one another.

Problem 2C: No Inline Editing

When a user spots an error during a high-pressure briefing, the system forces a context switch—navigating away to a separate form rather than allowing immediate correction.

"Imagine we're on a call and you just want to quickly, like, oh crap... even if there's a button that just says edit... I'm always looking for just ease."— Participant 3

Insight

Forcing navigation away from the view creates a workflow gap where the surveyor may abandon the digital tool in favor of a notebook.

Design Recommendations

Solutions
  • Add Day/Week/Month view toggles for broader temporal context
  • Introduce priority star indicators for high-risk items
  • Enable draggable task cards for dynamic reassignment
  • Implement quick-edit modals for in-context editing
03

Submission & Entry Review: Duplicate Confusion

Duplicate tabs with similar information and purpose caused decision friction. Users could not distinguish between "My Submissions" and "Entry Review" and overlooked the primary CTA.

Problem 3A: Confusing Tabs & Poor CTA Communication

Most users were unable to distinguish between My Submissions and Entry Review, questioned the complexity of the flow, and needed initial guidance. They also expected to navigate back but couldn't due to the absence of a back button.

"Submissions page and the entry review page, I feel like should be the same page. Those two things sound like the same thing to me."— Participant 7

Insight

4 out of 8 users faced confusion differentiating between these functionally similar tabs.

Problem 3B: Unclear Entry Review Structure

Under the 'Entry Review' tab, users were unable to clearly understand what was happening because two separate information sections (table and card) were displayed simultaneously, creating confusion and cognitive overload.

Insight

5 out of 8 users faced this issue, with some abandoning the task altogether.

Design Recommendations

Solutions
  • Consolidate My Submissions and Entry Review into a single "Submissions" tab
  • Make the primary CTA visually prominent with proper blue fill
  • Add checkboxes for bulk actions and row-specific action menus
  • Change table header color to differentiate from interactive elements
"I like a sleek platform, and it's really aesthetically pleasing to the eye. What I really like about this is having the different columns."

— Participant 3, noting positive aspects of the interface

The Outcome

From insights to implementation roadmap

Client Response

The MedLaunch team received our findings enthusiastically. Robert Dostert expressed that our solutions aligned with their development priorities and would be prioritized for near-term implementation.

Recommended Next Steps

Field testing is essential. Since surveyors use this tool while walking through hospitals—often with spotty connectivity—we recommended tablet-specific usability sessions in real clinical environments.

Key Learnings

This project reinforced that domain expertise doesn't compensate for interface issues. Even our healthcare-background participants struggled with workflows.

Impact Potential

Implementing these recommendations could move the Product Usability Scale score from 59.4 (Grade D) to the 70-80 range (Grade B-C) within one to two development cycles.
